Introduction

Cats (Felis catus) are one of the most popular domesticated animals in the world, with an estimated 600 million living alongside humans globally.

Domestic cats have lived with humans for approximately 10,000 years, originally attracted to early agricultural settlements by the abundance of rodents.

Despite thousands of years of domestication, cats retain many of the instincts and behaviors of their wild ancestors.

Cats are obligate carnivores, meaning their bodies require nutrients found only in animal tissue to survive and thrive.

A cat's brain is biologically more similar to a human brain than a dog's brain, sharing the same regions responsible for emotions.

Cats communicate with humans in ways they do not use with other cats, such as meowing, suggesting they adapt their behavior specifically for us.

There are over 70 recognized domestic cat breeds, ranging widely in coat type, body shape, temperament, and size.

The average lifespan of an indoor cat is 12 to 18 years, though many cats live well into their 20s with proper care.

Cats spend between 12 and 16 hours per day sleeping, making them one of the longest-sleeping mammals on Earth.

A group of cats is called a clowder, while a group of kittens born in the same litter is called a kindle.

    Anatomy

    Cats have a highly flexible skeleton with around 230 bones, compared to the 206 found in adult humans.

    A cat's spine is extremely flexible, containing more vertebrae than most mammals, which allows them to right themselves mid-fall — a reflex known as the "righting reflex."

    Cats have retractable claws on all paws except the dewclaw, which allows them to keep their claws sharp by protecting them from wear when not in use.

    A cat's whiskers, or vibrissae, are deeply rooted sensory organs that detect changes in airflow, helping cats navigate in the dark and judge whether they can fit through narrow spaces.

    The eyes of a cat have a vertical slit pupil that can open extremely wide in low light, giving cats excellent night vision roughly six times better than humans.

    Cats have a third eyelid called a nictitating membrane, which can close horizontally across the eye to protect and moisten it.

    A cat's tongue is covered with tiny backward-facing spines called papillae, which help strip meat from bones and act as a natural grooming brush.

    Cats have a total of 30 teeth as adults: 12 incisors, 4 canines, 10 premolars, and 4 molars.

    The paw pads of a cat contain sweat glands that leave scent marks when a cat scratches — one reason cats scratch furniture and posts.

    Cats have 32 muscles in each ear, allowing them to rotate their ears up to 180 degrees to pinpoint sounds with precision.

    Behavior

    Cats are crepuscular animals, meaning they are most active during twilight hours at dawn and dusk, though indoor cats often adjust their schedules to match their owners.

    Kneading, the rhythmic pushing of paws against a soft surface, is a behavior kittens use while nursing that many cats retain into adulthood as a sign of comfort.

    When a cat bumps its head against a person or object, called bunting, it is depositing scent from facial glands as a way of marking something as familiar and safe.

    Cats purr at a frequency between 25 and 150 Hz. Research suggests this frequency range may actually promote bone healing and reduce pain.

    A slow blink from a cat is considered a feline sign of trust and affection. Returning a slow blink to a cat has been shown to increase their willingness to approach humans.

    Cats are territorial animals that use scent, scratching, and spraying to mark their home range and communicate with other cats.

    Chirping and chattering — sounds cats make when watching birds or prey through a window — are thought to mimic the sounds of prey animals or express excitement and frustration.

    Cats often bring dead prey to their owners as a form of gift-giving behavior, believed to be inherited from the maternal instinct to teach hunting to offspring.

    A cat's tail position communicates mood: held high signals confidence and friendliness, tucked low signals fear or submission, and a puffed tail signals agitation or alarm.

    Cats typically prefer vertical space and elevated perches, as height gives them a sense of security and a better vantage point to observe their environment.

    Breeds

    The Persian is one of the oldest and most recognizable cat breeds, known for its long, luxurious coat, flat face, and calm, gentle temperament.

    The Maine Coon is the largest domestic cat breed, originating in North America, and is noted for its tufted ears, bushy tail, and dog-like personality.

    Siamese cats are one of the oldest Asian breeds, famous for their striking blue eyes, color-pointed coats, and highly vocal, social nature.

    The Sphynx is a hairless breed developed in Canada in the 1960s; despite lacking fur, they are considered warm to the touch due to their exposed skin radiating body heat.

    Bengal cats were developed by crossing domestic cats with Asian leopard cats, resulting in a breed with a distinctive spotted or marbled wild-looking coat.

    The Scottish Fold is recognized by its uniquely folded ears, caused by a cartilage mutation, giving the cat an owl-like facial appearance.

    Ragdoll cats are known for going limp when picked up, a trait that gives them their name, and are prized for their docile and affectionate temperament.

    The Abyssinian is a slender, athletic breed with a ticked coat and an active, curious, and playful personality often compared to that of a dog.

    British Shorthairs are a stocky, round-faced breed known for their dense plush coat, easygoing temperament, and loyalty to their families.

    The Norwegian Forest Cat is a large, semi-longhaired breed adapted to cold climates, featuring a thick double coat and tufted paws for navigating snow.

    Care

    Cats require a diet high in protein and fat derived from animal sources; they cannot synthesize the amino acid taurine on their own and must obtain it from food.

    Fresh water should always be available to cats. Many cats prefer running water and may drink more readily from pet fountains than still bowls.

    Indoor cats need environmental enrichment such as scratching posts, climbing trees, puzzle feeders, and interactive play to prevent boredom and behavioral issues.

    Cats should be spayed or neutered, typically around 4 to 6 months of age, to prevent unwanted litters and reduce the risk of certain cancers and behavioral issues.

    A clean litter box is essential; most cats prefer one litter box per cat plus one extra, scooped daily and fully changed at least once a week.

    Regular veterinary checkups, ideally once a year for young cats and twice a year for senior cats, help catch health issues early and keep vaccines current.

    Dental disease is one of the most common health problems in cats; daily tooth brushing with cat-safe toothpaste is the gold standard for oral care.

    Cats groom themselves extensively but longhaired breeds benefit from daily brushing to prevent matting and reduce hairballs.

    Microchipping is strongly recommended for all cats, even indoor ones, as it provides permanent identification if a cat escapes or gets lost.

    Keeping cats indoors significantly extends their lifespan, as outdoor cats face risks from traffic, predators, disease, and harsh weather conditions.
